Engineering College Life

Raw, real, honest, unbiased & complete — four years of engineering, told the way nobody tells it during admission season.

Author Amit Taksande Branch Computer Technology Journey PCE '22–'26 → IITK M.Tech '26–'28

So you're either about to start engineering, or you've already taken admission in a Tier-2 or Tier-3 college — maybe in 1st year, maybe in final year — wondering what the next few years actually look like. Maybe you're excited. Maybe you're nervous. Maybe you're comparing yourself to students from IITs and NITs, or simply trying to figure out whether engineering is worth the time, effort, and sacrifices you're about to make.

This is that guide.

I'll try to cover everything that genuinely matters — academics, skills, internships, placements, friendships, opportunities, mistakes, personal growth, and the reality of studying in a Tier-3 college. I'll also talk about things nobody tells you during admission season, but that end up having the biggest impact on your college life. Things not mentioned in the academic brochure.

One more thing — throughout my engineering journey, I didn't focus on just one path. Alongside college academics, I prepared for placements, government jobs, CAT and GATE at once. This was a major mistake. So everything here doesn't come from theory, YouTube motivation, or hearsay — it comes from my own experience, failures, and lessons learned.

I'll try my best to be 100% honest. No sugarcoating, no fake motivation, no unrealistic success stories, no drama. Just real experiences, observations, lessons, mistakes — and everything I wish someone had told me when I was in your position.

I know this is going to be a little long, but by the end, I'll try to make sure every second you spend reading it adds value to your engineering journey.

Section 1

Most Tier-3 Engineering Students Will Relate to This

First Year

The Honeymoon Period

Almost everyone enters college with excitement, curiosity, and a head full of expectations. Some students come from towns like Chandrapur, Gondia, Amravati; many are from Nagpur itself. Regardless of where they come from, most believe college is going to completely change their lives.

You'll meet every mindset imaginable: some convinced they'll graduate with the highest package, some already dreaming of starting the next big business, a few with clear plans for GATE or government jobs — and some who never wanted engineering in the first place but joined because their parents encouraged, or forced, them to.

This is usually when you make the most friends — hours in classrooms, canteens, practical labs, corridors. Some of these friendships last all four years; others vanish by next year.

Academically, everyone starts with enthusiasm. High CGPA, regular study, that famous 75% attendance — for a few weeks, notebooks are neat and motivation is sky-high.

But slowly, reality sets in. A large number of students eventually find themselves watching "Complete Unit in 15 Minutes" videos from YouTube the night before an exam. Somehow these become the unofficial professors for half the batch during exam season.

And many end up trying to impress that one person they find interesting.

Relationship advice: I'm honestly not the right person to give a fully balanced take here, but — do you want a partner because others have one, because you liked their beauty and not their heart, or because you want to show off how good they look? Be honest with yourself. There's no shame in being in a relationship, but from four years of watching this play out: 70–80% of relationships end by final year, and choosing the wrong person can end up wasting your entire four years. The decision is yours.
Second Year

Exploration Time

If first year was the honeymoon period, second year is where reality slowly starts knocking. You finally enter your branch with excitement, expecting to learn how real-world systems work and start feeling like an actual engineer.

For many, it doesn't go exactly as planned. Every subject is new — new terminology, new concepts, sometimes a completely different teaching style. There will be lectures where you sit for an hour and walk out wondering what was even taught. Many students hit their lowest CGPA somewhere in this phase, because the jump from first-year common subjects to branch-specific ones isn't always smooth.

At the same time, something interesting starts happening — you begin exploring your own boundaries. Some start coding, some prepare for competitive programming, some join technical events, a few discover GATE, only a handful think about MBA, government exams, higher studies, freelancing, startups, or internships — usually with very limited knowledge of what's actually right for them.

You'll notice everyone seems to be doing something different. One friend's into web dev, another's prepping for GATE, someone's into sports, someone else is already talking startups. (By the end of fourth year you'll realize — no one was really doing anything extraordinary. Everyone was just doing what they felt they were "supposed" to do.)

Second year is less about mastering a skill and more about discovering what genuinely interests you. You'll try things, quit some, fail at others, and hopefully land on one or two that stick.

"Next semester se pakka padhunga."
Third Year

The Wake-Up Call

This is usually when students realize how far behind they are in the placement race. The same people who spent two years saying "abhi toh time hai" suddenly discover placements aren't far away. Everyone seems to be learning something — DSA, projects, web development, aptitude, internships, referrals.

For many, this is when panic begins. You enroll in courses, build projects, fix your resume, try to understand what companies actually expect. Time feels like it's running faster than before. "How did two years pass so quickly?"

But third year isn't only about careers. This is also when many become active in technical clubs, cultural bodies, and student organizations — proudly updating LinkedIn with "Selected as XYZ Coordinator," hosting events, managing freshers' programs, getting closer to seniors and faculty.

Your friend circle shrinks — not from fights, but because everyone moves in a different direction. This is also the year of college trips, late-night career talks, group projects, internship applications, and a hundred conversations starting with "Bro, what are your plans after engineering?"

And then there's the largest group — students still living in a comfortable illusion. They know placements are coming, they know skills matter, they know competition exists — but they keep postponing action because somewhere deep down they believe there's still plenty of time.

Third year is probably the most important year of engineering. The habits, skills, and decisions made here shape your final year — and often your first job or higher-education path.
Final Year

The Final Chapter

Now everything feels like it's coming to an end. Nothing new left to discover — only unfinished battles to fight. Four years gone, and you're standing at the edge of graduation, suddenly.

This is when reality hits hardest. The final-year project begins, and everyone expects it to be unique, innovative — "you're an engineer, after all." But underneath, many students feel completely helpless, confidence fading, every decision feeling heavier.

Placement season arrives at the same time. Aptitude tests, coding rounds, interviews, lectures, assignments, exams — all at once. Every day a race against time, most days ending in confusion.

You start distancing from the friends who were once part of your everyday life — not from dislike, but because everyone is fighting their own battle now. The dreams from first year — the startup, the revolutionary app, becoming the next tech icon — collide with reality, suddenly much farther away.

And then comes the most painful thought:

"What if I am just average?"

After four years, many feel they have no extraordinary skill, no remarkable achievement, no clear direction. Confidence replaced by self-doubt and the weight of social pressure.

The dream package is gone. The dream company is gone. And suddenly the only option left seems to be a ₹4 LPA job.

But this is where most students misunderstand the story.

Final year is not the chapter where dreams end.
It is the chapter where illusions end.

At this point, many of you will start asking the difficult question:

"Did I make the right decision?"

After investing ₹12–13 lakhs (more, if you're a girl), four years, countless weekends, assignments, exams, and opportunities — is a ₹4 LPA job really worth it? And that's assuming you get placed at all. Five to ten years ago, colleges were giving the same package — after this much inflation and rising living costs, getting the same offer today means you need to think twice.

These questions are completely natural. Almost every final-year student asks them at some point. But before you jump to conclusions — take a deep breath. Hold on.

Section 3

Opportunities That Open Up After Engineering

Disclaimer: This section isn't for everyone — skip it if you've already decided what you want to do.

It's for those with a bigger aim in life — a good package.
It's for those who refuse to settle for an average life.
It's for those without a strong financial background.
It's for those hungry to work hard and change their circumstances.

If that's you — keep reading.
skip if not your priority Path 01

UPSC

The most famous and prestigious career path in India — but not for everyone. Not because you can't crack it, but because UPSC demands far more than intelligence: years of consistency, the right strategy, guidance, family support, and financial stability to survive the preparation phase. Many spend 4–5 years preparing for a single exam, and most don't clear it on the first few attempts.

One of my relatives has been preparing for 5–6 years and is still trying — and he's not even average, the exam itself is that competitive. Even candidates who become IPS officers can fail the Prelims on a repeat attempt. Choose UPSC only if you fully understand the risk and the uncertainty of the journey.

skip if not your priority Path 02

Railways, SSC, Banking & Other Government Jobs

I won't pretend to know every detail of every exam, but one thing's common: they all take time, patience, and consistent preparation. Competition is high — lakhs apply every year. But government jobs remain one of the most respected career options in India, offering a stable path for those willing to put in the effort.

Right now, with fewer responsibilities, private-sector salaries may look more attractive. But priorities change with time — many eventually value job security, work-life balance, and peace of mind over constant corporate pressure.

skip if not your priority Path 03

MBA

A good option — but mostly if you do it from a top college: IIMs, IITs (yes, they offer MBAs too), FMS Delhi, XLRI, SPJIMR. Getting in usually requires cracking CAT, which takes 1–2 years of serious preparation, and even then admission isn't guaranteed given the competition.

CAT is open to every background — B.Com, B.A., Medical, Engineering, CA, and more — which makes it tougher, especially for GEM (General Engineer Male) candidates. Even picking the "wrong" question set on exam day can swing your result. Most students at top MBA colleges aren't freshers either; many recommend gaining work experience first.

And the cost: a top MBA can run ₹20–30 lakh (₹30–35 lakh for the very top tier, over two years). Decide based on preparation time, admission stats, placement outcomes, and ROI — not assumptions.

skip if not your priority Path 04

MS Abroad

Opens up better research facilities, global exposure, higher salaries, and a career outside India — a great option if you want to explore opportunities beyond your borders.

I'd mainly recommend this if you have a strong financial background or a clear funding plan. Even before admission you may spend ₹3–4 lakh on exams, applications, coaching, and document processing — and total costs go far higher depending on the country and university.

Research rankings, tuition, scholarships, living costs, visa rules, job opportunities and ROI carefully before deciding.

Path 05

Off-Campus Placements

A completely different game. Not impossible — but definitely difficult. With the right competence, guidance, planning, and skills, it's absolutely doable; many students (maybe not from my college, but elsewhere) have landed great off-campus roles.

But I've also seen the other side. One of my batchmates — ranked #1 in our institute on GfG — still ended up with a ₹4 LPA job, still waiting on his joining letter (best wishes to him). Around 80% of my batchmates are still sitting at home. Some joined coaching institutes post-B.Tech to land a job, a few took a drop for GATE aiming for an IIT, some are even ready to take up sales jobs just to start earning — and living at home after B.Tech comes with a mental pressure you won't realize until you're there.

Most companies that visited our campus were sales or marketing-oriented; only a small fraction were actual IT companies. I'm not saying this to discourage anyone — with the right skills, guidance, and network, off-campus can absolutely work out better. Just do the research first, whatever path you choose.

Path 06 · hidden gem

GATE — Graduate Aptitude Test in Engineering

One of the most underrated opportunities for engineering students, especially from Tier-3 colleges. GATE is a direct pathway into IITs, NITs, and IISc. An M.Tech there usually costs only ₹2–3 lakh for two years, plus a stipend of ₹12,400/month — a huge financial relief. More importantly, it puts you in a room with some of the brightest minds in the country for two years, with a network and opportunities a Tier-3 college simply can't offer.

Many people leave their jobs just to prepare for GATE — you still have time during college. Moving from a Tier-3 college to a top IIT through GATE can be one of the biggest career upgrades possible, rather than settling for a ₹4 LPA job. The IIT tag helps — not because it guarantees success, but because it opens doors to better professors, alumni networks, and industry connections.

GATE also opens government job routes — PSUs, research orgs, ISRO, BARC, DRDO — many tied directly to GATE scores, especially for core branches. Even for CS, a top rank opens 50+ documented opportunities (credit to Rishi Bhaiya for that sheet). And unlike CAT, GATE is restricted to engineers only. Look at all the options and the actual data before deciding anything.

Section 4

Amit Bhaiya Wants to Tell You Something

After reading all this, many of you might be thinking — "I should start preparing for GATE or some other exam right now." I'd say: stop for a moment.

I always prefer data over motivation. For GATE CS, more than 2 lakh candidates register each year, but only a small fraction make it into the top IITs. Before choosing any path, do your own research — talk to students already at IITs, NITs, or working in the field you're aiming for. They have a far clearer picture than random voices on social media. There's no real GATE culture or encouragement at my college either — getting here was a different story altogether.

A last message for those who've already decided on GATE

I was once exactly where you are — confused, weighing every option. I've watched friends and seniors take drops for GATE and still struggle. The possibility of failure always exists. But one thing that gave me confidence was data, not assumptions.

If your goal is a top institute, don't just sit and study randomly — you need a plan, a strategy, and guidance from people who've already cracked it. You may not feel the weight of "guidance" now, but you will after your attempt. (And no — I'm not starting a mentorship program like other toppers.) If you're not ready to sit for long hours, consistently, for months — this path may not be for you. GATE isn't impossible, but wanting something bigger means being ready for bigger sacrifices.

With the right plan, strategy, and guidance — reaching a top institute is absolutely possible. Stay focused, stay consistent, solve problems, give yourself time. A few months — or 1–2 years — of real work can completely change the direction of your life.

Summary

The Short Version

If you're from PCE or any other Tier-3 college, and think that four years of hard work guarantees a basic ₹4 LPA on-campus job — first understand your college's actual placement reality, and come out of that illusion. Life after final year can be challenging, and it will hit hard.

So start exploring alternatives from your 2nd or 3rd year itself — off-campus placements, competitive exams, or higher education. Pursuing higher education from a good institute is often a better long-term investment than settling for a 5–6 LPA job right after graduation — but it demands complete dedication and consistent effort.

The sooner you understand this and start working toward your goals — whatever path you choose — make sure you understand its requirements, pros, and cons, not just for the next few years, but for the next 10–20. A well-informed decision today can shape your career, your finances, and your life.
